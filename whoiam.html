Exclusions from Patient Authorization for the Release of Information

Policy #1133-1

Effective Date: 12/1/2024

Created by: Suzanna Bolich

Approved by: Sally Smith, HIM Director

Citation of State and/or Federal Law

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996. Pub. L. 110. Stat. 1936.

United States, Office of the Assistant Secretary for Planning and Evaluation, HHS. "Standards for Privacy of Individually Identifiable Health Information." 65 Fed. Reg. 82802 (December 28, 2000)

Ohio State Legislature. House Bill 281 "Failure to report a crime or knowledge of a death or burn injury." ORC §2921.22 (April 6, 2023)

PURPOSE

Generally, disclosure of Protected Healthcare Information requires written consent from the patient, in accordance with the HIPAA Privacy Rule. However, there are specific situations where patient consent is not necessary. These exceptions are enumerated in 45 CFR §164.512. This policy will only address specific types of exceptions where we believe, in good faith, that it is necessary to prevent or lessen a serious and imminent threat to the health or safety of a person or the public.

POLICY

Public Health

PHI may be disclosed to public health authorities for the prevention of disease, injury, or disability. This includes disclosures to the FDA with respect to food, dietary supplements, or product defects.

Victims of Crime

We are required by law to report any suspicions of child abuse or neglect, contact the Social Services Department immediately.

If you reasonably believe an adult patient to be a victim of abuse, neglect, or domestic violence, and believe disclosure would prevent serious harm, you should inform your supervisor to determine the appropriate course of action. It is important to note that we are legally required to inform the victim of any report made unless doing so could result in possible harm. Furthermore, ORC § 2921.22 defines a legal obligation to notate any reasonable suspicion of domestic violence, and the basis for that suspicion, in the patient's records.

If a law enforcement official requests information about an individual who is suspected to be a victim of a crime, we may disclose PHI only if the individual cannot consent due to emergency or incapacitation, provided that:

  1. The information is not intended to be used against the victim.
  2. Immediate law enforcement activity depends upon the disclosure.
  3. We determine the disclosure is in the best interests of the individual.

Other Rules Regarding Law Enforcement

We must disclose PHI if it is necessary for the identification or apprehension of an individual that has admitted to participation in a violent crime, or if the individual has escaped from lawful custody.

We are legally required to report knowledge of a felony, and certain types of wounds or other physical injuries. Specifically, we must report to law enforcement any gunshot or stab wound, serious burns, any serious physical harm to persons resulting from violence, any harm caused by "fireworks," or harm caused by an explosive or incendiary device.

We must release PHI in compliance with a court order or court-ordered warrant. We may disclose PHI in limited forms if presented with a subpoena or otherwise requested by law enforcement if:

  1. The information sought is material for an investigation.
  2. The request is specific and limited.
  3. De-identified information cannot be used.

These are complex issues and should be handled with unmitigated care and sensitivity. If you are unsure about the appropriateness of disclosure in any situation, contact the Compliance Office or HIM Department for assistance. Any conduct found to be in violation of any law, statute, or regulation may result in disciplinary action.

Release of Information from an Alcohol and Drug Treatment Facility

Policy #1133-2

Effective Date: 12/1/2024

Created by: Suzanna Bolich

Approved by: Sally Smith, HIM Director

Citation of State and/or Federal Law

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996. Pub. L. 110. Stat. 1936.

United States, Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration, HHS. "Confidentiality of Substance Use Disorder Patient Records." 82 Fed. Reg. 6052 (January 18, 2017)

PURPOSE

The privacy of our clients is of the utmost importance to our organization. In addition to our own principles, there are legal conditions we must fulfill. The two primary laws governing privacy and confidentiality restrictions with respect to patient information are HIPAA and 42 CFR Part 2, "Confidentiality of Substance Use Disorder Patient Records." The purpose of this policy is to ensure we maintain the highest standards of confidentiality and maintain strict adherence to legal requirements regarding the disclosure of Protected Health Information. Carefully review this document and contact compliance or HIM Department staff if you have any questions. Any conduct found to be in violation of any law, statute, or regulation may result in disciplinary action.

POLICY

Please note, we are required by law to provide our clients with written notice of our privacy policies and procedures. When disclosure of Protected Healthcare Information is necessary, we must first obtain written consent from the patient, in accordance with the HIPAA Privacy Rule. We should assist clients when consent is necessary, to ensure the completeness of the authorization provided. To conform to legal guidelines the consent to release PHI must include the following elements:

  • Name of the patient.
  • Purpose of the disclosure.
  • Name of the organization or person making the disclosure.
  • The name of the recipient (individual or organization) of the disclosure.
  • A specific description of the information to be disclosed (how much and what kind).
  • Signature of the patient, a parent, or guardian.
  • The date the patient signed the consent.
  • The date, the event, or condition upon which consent will expire if consent has not been previously revoked.
  • A statement regarding the potential for the records used or disclosed pursuant to the consent to be subject to redisclosure by the recipient and no longer protected.
  • A statement explaining the consequences to the patient of a refusal to sign the consent.
  • The following statement: "This consent is subject to revocation at any time except to the extent that the program has already acted on it."

Pursuant to 42 CFR Part 2, there are limited exceptions to these authorization requirements, including the following:

  • Medical Emergencies
  • Scientific Research
  • Management audits, financial audits, and program evaluations
  • Disclosures for public health

If you believe consent may not be necessary in a situation, please inform your supervisor to determine the appropriate course of action.

Why This Matters

This work shows I can navigate complex legal and regulatory environments. HIPAA and 42 CFR Part 2 aren't simple, but I can explain them clearly and build practical organizational procedures around them.

It demonstrates commitment to accuracy in high-stakes situations. Release of Information decisions directly impact patient privacy and legal compliance. I don't take shortcuts or make assumptions, I verify against actual regulatory language and provide clear decision-making frameworks.

Organizations need HIM professionals who can read regulations, understand their implications, and translate them into actionable policies that protect both patients and the organization from legal liability.
